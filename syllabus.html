Target Audience

The “reluctant coder”: a graduate student in conventional engineering (MechSE, MatSE, CEE, NPRE, etc.) with a need to write and manage research codes, for instance at a national laboratory or in academia. This demographic rarely has the prerequisite courses required to take upper-level CS courses.

Prerequisites include: CS 420 Parallel programming or equivalent exposure to HPC concepts (although these will be reviewed as well); CS 450 Numerical analysis or TAM 470 Computational mechanics or equivalent exposure to numerical concepts.

Objectives

To teach students the components and methods of modern scientific software development by exposure to lectures and hands-on tutorials exemplifying component principles. The workshop days will focus on:

  1. the basis and elements of modern scientific software and the development process;

  2. the integration of a modern numerical library, PETSc;

  3. the development of a simple multiphysics code using the MOOSE Framework (built on top of PETSc).

Projects

Each project will have clearly defined goals, specifications, and benchmarks. The projects will focus on the translation of model concepts into numerics and algorithms as well as providing exposure to the software development concepts discussed in the workshop.

Development for PETSc and MOOSE should take place on a regular serial platform[ref], and we will provide either a VM or compilation support for those interested. For execution, the PETSc and MOOSE libraries will be installed and supported on Blue Waters as well as Campus Cluster.
